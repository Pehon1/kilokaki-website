Why Calorie Counts Are Wrong (And What Actually Matters for Your Goals)

The calorie on the label is already 20% wrong. Here's why obsessing over exact numbers is a waste of time.

The nutrition label on your chicken breast says 165 calories. Nutritional science consistently shows real-world intake is estimated within ±20% even by trained dietitians using food databases. The actual chicken breast you bought could be 132 calories. It could be 198. You weighed it. You logged it. You were careful. And the label was already lying to you by nearly a third.

Here's what most food loggers do: they become spreadsheet people. They weigh every gram. They agonise over whether "medium" apple meant 150g or 180g. They import barcode data. They build macro ratios into their training splits. And then they wonder why they feel more stressed about lunch than about work.

The obsession with precision creates a specific problem: it makes logging feel like a chore. And when logging feels like a chore, you stop.

Research on successful weight management keeps pointing to the same variable: consistency of tracking, not precision of measurement. One study in Preventive Medicine found people who logged food daily — even with rough estimates — lost more weight than people who logged precisely twice a week. The act of logging is the intervention. The number is just data.

If you log 80% of what you eat, consistently, you'll have a better outcome than someone who meticulously tracks 100% for two weeks and then quits. Most people who "fail" at food tracking aren't failing because they estimated 400 calories instead of 380. They're failing because the system required too much overhead.
