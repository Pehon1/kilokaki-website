Why You're Always Hungry (And What to Actually Fix)

Hunger during a diet isn't a character flaw. It's a signal your body is sending because something in your food choices needs to change.

You've been eating "less" for a week. The calories are down. The scale might be moving slightly. But by 3pm you're staring at the office biscuit tin like it owes you money.

You assume it's willpower. It's not. It's food composition.

Hunger is driven by hormones, gut signals, and the physical properties of the food you eat — not just the number on your plate. Here's what's actually going wrong, and what to do about it.

1. You're Not Eating Enough Protein

Protein is the most satiating macronutrient. Not because of some fad — because of basic biology. It triggers the release of peptide YY and GLP-1, hormones that tell your brain you're full. It also slows gastric emptying, meaning food sits in your stomach longer.

Most people eating "less" end up eating the same types of food — carb-heavy, protein-light. A chicken breast and a bowl of rice have very different effects on how full you feel two hours later.

"Protein triggers the release of peptide YY and GLP-1 — hormones that tell your brain you're full. Carbs don't do this."

The fix: Aim for 1.6–2.2g of protein per kg of bodyweight. That's roughly 100–150g for a 70kg person. Prioritise protein at every meal — eggs at breakfast, lean meat or fish at lunch and dinner.

2. Fiber Is Missing

Fiber adds bulk to food without adding calories. It slows digestion, feeds gut bacteria (which produce satiety signals), and physically fills your stomach. High-fiber foods — vegetables, legumes, whole grains — create volume for very few calories.

The problem: most "diet foods" strip fiber out. Low-fat yogurt, white rice, protein bars with maltitol — they're calorie-reduced but also fiber-reduced. Your stomach is emptier and your gut isn't getting the signal.

The fix: Add one extra serve of vegetables or legumes to every meal. Switch white rice to brown or add a side of leafy greens. Track your fiber — KiloKaki logs it, and you'll see most people are running at half the recommended 25-30g/day.

3. You're Eating the Same Volume

Your stomach has mechanoreceptors. When it stretches, you feel full. When it doesn't, you don't — regardless of how many calories you've eaten.

A 500-calorie salad with grilled chicken, avocado, and mixed vegetables fills you differently than a 500-calorie sandwich with white bread and mayo. Same calories. Very different stomach experience.

The fix: Build meals around high-volume, lower-calorie foods: salads, soups (not cream-based), stir-fries, grilled proteins with vegetables. Use a smaller plate. Start meals with vegetables or protein before carbs.

4. Blood Sugar Swings

Eat a high-glycemic meal — white rice, sugary cereal, a muffin — and your blood glucose spikes. Insulin follows. Glucose gets cleared. Blood sugar drops. Your brain reads this as: you need more food. You feel hungry again within 90 minutes.

This isn't a diabetes thing. It happens to everyone. It's just more noticeable when you're already in a calorie deficit.

The fix: Combine carbs with protein or fat to slow absorption. Don't eat rice alone — add chicken, tofu, or eggs. Snack on something with fat or protein, not just fruit or crackers.

The Pattern

Most people trying to eat less make the same mistake: they eat less food instead of different food. They cut portions but keep the same carb-heavy, fiber-light, protein-sparse composition.

The solution isn't smaller meals. It's meals that work harder for your satiety.
