How to Log Your Grandmother's Recipes Without a Recipe Book

No recipe? No problem. KiloKaki's voice and photo logging let you log family meals, hawker food, and dishes you know by heart — without a single nutritional label.

4 min read

You know the taste by heart. You have no idea how many calories are in it.

That's the recipe problem. Every food logging app assumes you have a recipe — a list of ingredients, a nutritional label, something to look up. But the meals that matter most — your grandmother's curry, your mum's braised pork, the hawker stall you've been eating at since you were twelve — don't come with nutritional labels. They come with memory.

And that's exactly why people stop logging food. Not because they're lazy. Because the thing they actually eat doesn't fit into the app.

The estimation problem nobody addresses

Most food logging apps are built for people who cook from recipes or buy from chains with calorie counts. They're not built for people who eat food that's cooked by feel, by tradition, by someone who learned from someone else who learned from someone before that.

When you try to log "my grandmother's chicken curry" into a standard app, you hit a wall immediately. How many grams of chicken? What kind of oil did she use? Was the coconut cream full-fat or light? You don't know. The app doesn't help. So you either guess (and feel wrong about it) or you skip logging entirely.

The problem isn't you. The problem is the system.

Why estimates are more honest than guessing

Here's the thing about guessing: you probably do it already. Every meal you've ever logged with a "close enough" entry was a guess. The question isn't whether you estimate — it's whether you're honest about it.

KiloKaki is honest about it. When you use voice logging to describe "a big plate of lor mee with extra ngoh hiang from the stall at Geylang," KiloKaki doesn't ask you to break it into ingredients. It gives you an estimate based on what you described.

That estimate is more accurate than a manual entry built on guesswork, because it's based on real dish data — not on you trying to remember every ingredient while simultaneously trying to remember portion sizes.

You don't need to be a nutritionist. You just need to describe what you ate.

The real win: consistency over perfection

Here's what most people miss about food logging: the habit matters more than the precision.

A log that's 80% accurate and complete is infinitely more useful than a log that's 100% accurate for one week and then abandoned.

The problem with perfectionism in food logging is that it kills the habit. You miss a meal. The log looks incomplete. You feel guilty. You stop opening the app.

Voice logging family food sidesteps this entirely. Because it's fast — twenty seconds, not five minutes of database scrolling — you actually do it. The log gets kept. The habit forms.

And once the habit forms, the data becomes genuinely useful. You see patterns. You notice that weeks when you eat more home cooking have different energy levels than weeks of hawker food every day. You learn what works for your body without needing a nutrition degree.

How to estimate dishes you've eaten 200 times

You don't need to be precise. You need to be consistent.

The portion size guide:

• Small bowl / half a plate = roughly 1 serving
• Regular plate / one helping = 1–1.5 servings depending on the dish
• Extra portion, seconds, or "more than usual" = 1.5–2 servings
• A bit / a taste / just a few bites = half a serving or less

The preparation guide:

• Stir-fried = assume oil is used, portion is standard
• Braised / stewed = typically higher calorie density per bite
• Steamed / boiled / blanched = typically lower calorie density
• Deep-fried = double the oil estimate, add 20–30% calories

The combo guide:

• Rice + 2 dishes = estimate the dishes, rice is roughly 200–250kcal per cup
• Noodles in soup = estimate the noodles + toppings, broth is mostly negligible
• Mixed platters (yao taio, ngoh hiang) = estimate per piece, most are 80–120kcal each

None of this is exact. All of it is useful.
