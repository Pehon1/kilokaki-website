Why Most Apps Lose You in Week 1

Every food logging app looks the same in Week 1. Clean interface. Cheerful onboarding. A progress bar that tells you to log breakfast.

Then Week 2 hits.

The novelty fades. Logging starts to feel like homework. The calorie counter tells you you're over target and you don't even know why. The barcode scanner can't find your hawker centre chicken rice. You give up.

This is the pattern. Not just for KiloKaki — for every food logging app. Retention studies across the category show 60–70% of new users gone by Day 7.

But some people stay. And they don't just stay — they actually build the habit. They log food every day without it feeling like a chore. After six months, it's just part of their routine, like brushing teeth.

Why?

What the People Who Stick Have in Common

We looked at our own data. Not the vanity metrics — the behavioral patterns. What do the people who log consistently for 3, 4, 5 months actually do differently?

1. They log before they overthink.

The biggest predictor of sustained usage isn't motivation — it's capture speed. People who stick with KiloKaki log food within 30 seconds of eating. Not because they're more disciplined. Because they removed the friction that used to slow them down.

Voice logging matters here. Saying "toward chicken rice" takes 4 seconds. Opening a calorie counter app, finding the right entry, adjusting portion size — that takes 2 minutes. The people who stick chose the 4-second version, every time, until it became automatic.

2. They use templates after the first week.

Once they figured out what they ate regularly, they stopped typing and started templating. Same breakfast every weekday? One template. Same gym lunch? One template. The habit got faster because they stopped rebuilding it every time.

This is the product working as intended. KiloKaki's template system wasn't designed for power users — it was designed for people on their 8th consecutive day of logging who were starting to look for shortcuts. The system gave them one.

3. They edit without guilt.

Here's the thing about food logging that nobody talks about: you're going to be wrong about portions. A lot.

The people who stick don't let imperfect logs demotivate them. They correct. They adjust. They use the edit flow freely because KiloKaki makes it fast — 108 edits per 100 food logs in our current cohort. That's not a bug. That's the product teaching people that accuracy compounds over time, not perfection on day one.

And when they need to correct? Voice editing makes that fast too. The voice nudge fires at exactly the right moment — after 2 photo logs with no voice usage. The user has started the habit, is still in the novelty window, and the nudge offers a faster path without interrupting the flow. 93% of users who see it try voice logging within 24 hours.
