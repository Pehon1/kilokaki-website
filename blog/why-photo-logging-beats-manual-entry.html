Why Photo Logging Beats Manual Entry

Photo logging is already the dominant input at KiloKaki — 54% of all logs. Here's why snapping a plate beats typing every entry, and why your edits are the feature, not the bug.

5 min read

You just ate lunch. The plate is in front of you — nasi lemak, half demolished. You could spend two minutes typing it into an app: "nasi lemak, 1 scoop rice,半个 egg, satu分包 teh-o peng." Then search for it. Find a close match. Adjust the portions. Maybe add the fish curry. You know roughly what it has in it but the app doesn't, so you approximate.

Or you could take a photo and send it.

Eight seconds. Done.

What photo logging actually does

Photo logging isn't "snap and forget." It's a different workflow:

1. You take the photo. This is the capture. Everything you ate, in one image.

2. The AI reads it. It estimates the macros based on what's in the image — portion sizes, composition, probable ingredients. This is where the AI earns its keep.

3. You correct it. You see what it estimated. You fix what was wrong. Maybe it missed the extra fishcake. Maybe the chicken portion was smaller than it guessed. You edit. Takes 20 seconds.

The total workflow: photo, review, edit, save. Under a minute for a full mixed meal. Versus two to three minutes of typing and searching in a database that probably doesn't have "uncle's nasi lemak with extra sambal" anyway.

The edit rate isn't a problem

Some people worry when they hear that most photo logs get edited. 86% of photos at KiloKaki get at least one correction after the AI reads them. They think: "Doesn't that mean the AI is wrong most of the time?"

No. It means the workflow is working exactly as designed.

The AI gives you a starting estimate. You have the final say. The edit isn't a failure — it's the part where you add your actual knowledge ("less rice than usual") to the AI's reasonable guess ("medium rice portion"). Together you get accurate data. The AI learns from your corrections over time too.

The edit is where you turn "what the AI thought you ate" into "what you actually ate." That's the whole point.

Why manual entry loses

Manual entry asks you to translate a meal into numbers before you've even eaten it. You search for the food. You pick from a database that has 90% coverage of packaged food and maybe 20% of what you actually eat — hawker meals, mixed dishes, the thing your mum made without a recipe.

Even when you find something close, you have to estimate the portion. "Medium" is not a unit of measurement. You know your "medium" isn't the same as the database's "medium." But you pick it anyway because it's the closest option.

Photo logging skips the translation step. You show the plate. The AI sees what's on it. You correct what it missed. The data is based on what you actually ate — not an estimate from a database entry that was close enough.

What the numbers look like

At KiloKaki, over half of all food logs are photos. Not because typing is hard — because photo logging is faster and more accurate for mixed dishes, hawker food, and anything that doesn't come from a package.

The users who log by photo consistently log more meals per week than users who type. Not because they're more disciplined. Because the task is easier. Logging a photo takes less effort than building a manual entry from scratch every time.

Lower friction → more logging → better data → more useful.

Photo logging works on real meals

The thing about databases is they need the food to already exist in them. For packaged food, that's fine — barcode scans, nutrition labels, done. For everything else — hawker food, home cooking, the random thing you ordered — the database has nothing. You build the entry yourself, which takes time and often ends with "close enough."

Photo logging doesn't care if the food exists in a database. It looks at what's on your plate and makes an estimate. Char kway teow, mixed vegetables, a bowl of soup with fish — all estimable from an image. None of them require a pre-existing database entry.

That's why photo logging works for Singapore food in a way that manual entry never will. The database will never have "uncle's economic rice with two dishes, less oil." The camera doesn't need it to.

Your next meal

Compare how long it took to how long a manual entry would've taken. And compare the accuracy — the photo estimate is based on what's actually on your plate, not what you approximated from a search result.

The edit isn't extra work. It's the part where the data becomes yours.

Photo logging isn't about avoiding typing. It's about capturing what you actually ate, not what you managed to search for.
