How to Log Your Grandmother's Recipes Without a Recipe Book

Hawker food, home cooking, and family recipes don't come with nutrition labels. Here's how to log them anyway — using your voice, your eyes, and approximations that are close enough.

5 min read

Your grandmother doesn't measure anything. She cooks by feel, by memory, by the sound of the wok. She makes char kway teow the way her mother made it, which is the way her mother's mother made it. There's no recipe card. There are no nutrition facts. There is only the plate in front of you, and it's delicious.

And you want to log it.

The problem with most food logging apps is they expect food to come in a package. Barcode → nutrition label → done. That works for cereal and protein bars. It doesn't work for anything your grandmother made. Or anything you got from the hawker centre. Or anything you ordered that came without an ingredient list.

KiloKaki was built for exactly this situation.

Voice logging: describe what you ate

The fastest way to log hawker food or home cooking is voice. You literally tell it what you ate:

"Char kway teow, extra fishcake, less noodles than usual, one teh-o peng."

Send it. Done. The AI reads your description and logs approximate macros based on the components you named. It doesn't need a database entry for "uncle's char kway teow from the stall on the corner." It needs the components: noodle, fishcake, sauce. You gave it those.

This works for anything you can describe. "Vegetarian bee hoon, no mock meat, clear soup, half portion." "Chicken rice, steamed not roasted, extra cucumber, less peanut sauce." "Mum's curry — chicken, potato, carrot, coconut milk."

The more specific you are, the better the estimate. But even rough descriptions work fine. The point isn't precision to the decimal — it's consistency over time. If you log "mum's curry" the same way every time, you'll see the pattern of your actual intake.

Photo logging: show the plate

When you can't describe it precisely enough — or you're not sure what's in it — take a photo. Send it to KiloKaki. The AI looks at the image and estimates the macros based on portion size, composition, and probable ingredients.

This is especially useful for mixed dishes where the components are hard to separate: a plate of economic rice with three dishes, a bowl of wanton noodles with extra sauce, a sambal stingray with lots of那个 sambal.

Photo logging is fast — eight seconds to take the shot and send. And you don't need to know what's in it. The AI figures out approximately what's there from the image.

Photo logging works on food that has no label, no barcode, and no database entry. It works on exactly the food your grandmother cooks.

When in doubt, estimate

One of the most useful skills for food logging is learning to estimate portions visually. You don't need a scale. You don't need measuring cups. You just need to develop a sense of what a portion actually looks like.

A portion of protein — chicken, fish, tofu — is roughly the size of your palm (without fingers). A portion of carbs — rice, noodles — is roughly what fits in two cupped hands. A portion of vegetables is roughly what fits in your two hands together, cupped.

You don't say "150 grams of chicken breast." You say "one palm-sized piece of chicken." That's enough. Log it that way. Over time, you get a consistent picture of your actual intake — not perfectly precise, but consistent. And consistency is more useful than precision.

The database problem doesn't exist here

Every other food logging app has the same problem: the database is full of packaged food and restaurant chains. It's empty for hawker food, home cooking, and anything made without a recipe.

KiloKaki sidesteps this entirely. Instead of searching a database, you describe or photograph what you ate. The AI estimates based on what you told it or showed it. The database doesn't need to have "ahLKee's chicken curry" because you're not searching for it — you're describing it.

This is the fundamental difference. Other apps ask: "Can we find this food in our database?" KiloKaki asks: "Can you describe what you ate?" The second question has a much better answer.

What to log and how

You don't need to log everything perfectly. You need to log the things that matter most — the main meals, the components you care about. Here's a practical guide:

For hawker food: Use voice. Name the dish and the key components. "Chicken rice, steamed, extra cucumber, no peanut sauce." Log it after you eat, while the plate is still in front of you and you can remember what was actually on it.

For home cooking: Use whatever method captures the meal fastest. If it's a simple dish, voice works. If it's a complex multi-component dish you're not sure about, take a photo.

For family recipes: This is where voice really shines. "Mum's curry — chicken, potato, carrot, coconut milk, extra potato." You know what's in it. Say it. Log it. Over time, "mum's curry" becomes a tracked entry with consistent estimates, and you can see how it fits into your week.

The point

The goal is not to log perfectly. The goal is to log consistently so you have actual data instead of impressions. Your grandmother's recipes are some of the most nourishing food you eat — they deserve to be in your log too, not because you need precision, but because you deserve to see the full picture of your eating.

Voice and photo make that possible. No database required.

Your grandmother never wrote down her recipes. Your log doesn't need her to either. Describe what you ate. Move on.
