MEAL PLANNING

The Meal Planning Method: Log Today, Plan Tomorrow

Meal planning doesn't have to mean spreadsheets and Sunday batch cooking. Here's a simple method that works around your real life — and how food logging makes it easier every week.

6 min read

Here's the honest truth about meal planning: most of it fails not because the plan is bad, but because the plan is too ambitious.

You don't need a colour-coded spreadsheet. You don't need to prep seven lunches on a Sunday afternoon. You just need to know what's coming, and log the gap between what you planned and what actually happened.

That's a real meal planning method. Here's how to run it.

The one-day-at-a-time planning method

Instead of planning a full week, plan one day ahead. Tonight, think about tomorrow:

  • Breakfast: will I eat at home or grab something?
  • Lunch: will it be meal-prepped or hawker centre?
  • Dinner: cooking or ordering?

This takes five minutes. You're not committing to specific dishes. You're just setting expectations.

Then you log what actually happens versus what you planned. That's the feedback loop.

Real example: you planned to eat at home. It rained. You ordered laksa. You log: "planned: home-cooked chicken rice. reality: laksa from the hawker centre." That's the whole point — the gap between plan and reality is the data.

Over two weeks, you'll see patterns:

  • You always underestimate how much you snack after dinner
  • Lunch at the hawker centre is consistently higher calorie than you think
  • Your "I'll eat at home" plan fails three days a week

The data doesn't judge. It just shows you the pattern so you can decide whether to change it.

How food logging makes weekly planning easier

Here's what most people miss: the log from last week is the best planning tool you have.

Look at your seven-day log. Ask:

  • Which dinners kept me full until the next morning?
  • Which lunches left me starving by 3pm?
  • Where were the actual calories coming from — main meals or between-meal snacking?

Last week's log is a meal review. It's data on what actually worked, not what you intended to work.

Use it:

  • If you felt full after dinner on Wednesday but starving by 10pm on Thursday, what's different? Wednesday was a protein-heavy dish. Thursday was a rice-heavy dish with less protein.
  • If you consistently over-order at dinner but not at lunch, that's a willpower problem in the evening — not a planning problem.

You can't fix what you can't see. Food logging makes the invisible visible.

The double-portions method

Not everyone has time to meal prep on Sunday. But most people can do this:

Cook two portions every time you cook.

Whatever you're making for dinner, make enough for lunch the next day. That's it.

Over a week, that's 3-4 meal-prepped lunches without any dedicated prep session.

The math is simple: if you're already cooking, adding one extra portion takes almost no additional effort. The time cost is in the cooking session itself — not in the portion size. Four containers of balanced meals take 20 minutes to put together if you're already at the stove.

This isn't glamorous. But it works around your real life.

The weekly review: 10 minutes on Sunday

Set a weekly review habit: Sunday night, spend 10 minutes on your food log for the past week.

  • What was the pattern? (snacking at night, high calorie lunch days, missed breakfasts)
  • What should stay in next week's plan? (the lunch that actually kept you full)
  • What do you want to change? (not "eat better" — something specific, like "reduce evening snack to one portion")

Write one intention for the week. Not five. One.

Then log, review, repeat.

Why this method won't overwhelm you

Most meal planning fails because it adds a planning task to an already full schedule. You have to think about food before you eat it, shop for specific ingredients, prep in a specific way on a specific day.

This method integrates with what you're already doing:

  • You eat anyway — so log what you eat
  • You cook sometimes — so double the portions
  • You have intentions for the week — so write one specific change instead of a full week's menu

The system works around your life. It doesn't demand a life designed around it.
