How Much Protein Do You Actually Need

Most people eat half the protein they need and don't realise it. Here's how to figure out what you actually need, and how to get more of it without becoming a bodybuilder.

6 min read

Think about your last meal. What was on the plate?

If you're in Singapore, it's probably rice. Or noodles. Or bread. Something carbohydrate-heavy, filling the centre of the plate. The protein — the chicken, the fish, the egg — is the side.

That's backwards.

The number nobody knows

How much protein do you need in a day? Most people have no idea. They know they "should eat more protein." They don't know what that means in grams, in meals, in food.

The rough guide: 1.6g to 2.2g of protein per kilogram of body weight per day, if you're trying to build or maintain muscle. Less if you're sedentary. More if you're training hard. For a 65kg person, that's roughly 100-140g of protein a day.

To give you a reference point: a typical Singapore hawker meal — chicken rice, one serving — has about 30g of protein. A plate of char kway teow, maybe 20g. An egg, 6g. A glass of Milo, 2g.

Most people are eating 60-80g of protein a day when they need 100g or more. The gap is real, and it shows up as hunger, low energy in the afternoons, and muscle loss over time.

Protein is the only macronutrient your body can't store. What you eat today is what you have today. No reserve account.

Why protein keeps you full

Protein is the most satiating macronutrient. Fat is a close second. Carbohydrates — especially refined ones — are the least satiating per calorie. This is why you can eat a big bowl of noodles and be hungry again in two hours. The rice digested fast, your blood sugar spiked and dropped, and your body is looking for more.

Protein triggers the release of GLP-1 and peptide YY — hormones that signal fullness. It also takes more energy to digest than carbs. Eating 30g of protein burns about 10-15 calories in digestion. Eating 30g of carbs burns almost none.

What this means practically: if you front-load protein at breakfast and lunch — not as an afterthought, but as the centre of the plate — you eat less overall without thinking about it.

The practical target

Here's a simple framework: aim for 25-30g of protein per meal, across three meals. That's 75-90g. Add a protein-rich snack if you're active or training, and you're at 100g+ easily.

What does 25-30g look like?

Breakfast: Two eggs (12g) + a serving of cereal with milk (8g) = 20g. Add a scoop of protein powder or a piece of cheese, you're at 30g.

Lunch: A chicken breast (180g) = 40g. Add a dollop of Greek yoghurt = another 10g.

Dinner: A piece of fish (150g) = 30g. Add tofu (100g) = another 15g.

This is not a bodybuilding diet. This is a normal person eating real food, with protein as the anchor of each meal.

The logging advantage

Here's where food logging helps: you find out immediately if you're short.

You eat a plate of economic rice — two dishes, rice, maybe 15g of protein — and you log it. KiloKaki tells you: that was 15 grams. You needed 25 for this meal. You're 10 short.

Now you know. You can add an egg. A glass of milk. Some tofu. Whatever fits the meal. Without logging, you just... don't know. You eat, you move on, and you wonder why you're hungry at 3pm.

Logging doesn't judge. It informs. And once you know the protein content of the food you actually eat — not estimated, not guessed, but logged — you can actually adjust.
